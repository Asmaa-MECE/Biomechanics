Section 1

What Defines Running?

The defining characteristic of running is the flight phase — a period during which neither foot is in contact with the ground. This aerial phase is entirely absent in walking.

Exam Point

Flight phase is the key biomechanical feature that distinguishes running from walking. If there is no flight phase, it is not running.

Because of this flight phase, running requires a fundamentally different mechanical model from walking. While walking is an inverted pendulum, running is best described as a spring-mass system.

Key Distinction

In running, potential and kinetic energy rise and fall together (in phase). In walking they are out of phase — the core reason walking is efficient at low speeds.

Section 3

Running Speed

Running speed depends on two variables:

Speed = Stride Length × Stride Frequency
v = Lstride · fstride

Increasing Speed

  • Increasing stride length — longer distance covered per stride.
  • Increasing stride frequency — more strides taken per second.
  • Elite performance — the best runners improve both simultaneously.
Section 4

Running Gait Cycle

The running gait cycle consists of two phases. Unlike walking, the stance phase is shorter and there is no double support — instead there is a flight phase.

Stance Phase (~40%)

The foot contacts the ground. Functions: support body weight, store elastic energy, generate propulsion.

Flight Phase (~60%)

Both feet are off the ground. Functions: forward progression, energy conservation, preparation for the next stance.

Stance ~40%
Flight ~60%
Stance phase
Flight phase
Comparison with Walking

Walking is ~60% stance, ~40% swing, with double support. Running is ~40% stance and ~60% flight, with no double support at all.

Section 5

Ground Reaction Forces in Running

Ground reaction forces are much larger during running than walking. The vertical GRF supports body weight and redirects movement.

Vertical GRF

Typical peak values during running reach 2–3 times body weight, compared with approximately 1× body weight during walking. The running GRF shows a single large peak, rather than the double-hump of walking.

Consequences of Large Impact Forces

  • Stress fractures
  • Tendon injuries
  • Joint degeneration
  • Overuse injuries
Section 6

The Spring-Mass Model

The spring-mass model is the most important concept in Chapter 3. During running the leg behaves like a spring and the body behaves like a mass riding on top of it.

Figure 3.2 — Spring-mass model: body = mass, leg = spring; force F = kx where k is stiffness and x is compression
3.2

During Early Stance

The leg compresses, storing elastic energy — analogous to a spring being pushed down.

During Late Stance

The leg recoils, releasing stored energy — propelling the body upward and forward into the flight phase.

Spring Force Equation

F = kx, where F is the spring force, k is the leg stiffness, and x is the compression distance. Greater stiffness produces greater force for the same compression.

Section 7

Running Energetics

Running efficiency comes largely from elastic energy storage and return. The following structures act as biological springs:

  • Achilles tendon — primary elastic energy store in the lower leg
  • Foot arch — deforms and recoils with each step
  • Tendons and muscles — contribute to elastic return
1
LandingElastic energy is stored in tendons and muscles as leg compresses.
2
Mid-StanceLeg at maximum compression; maximum elastic energy stored.
3
Push-OffElastic energy released as leg recoils, propelling body forward.
4
FlightBody moves efficiently through the air; muscles recover for next cycle.
Key Difference from Walking

In walking, PE and KE are out of phase. In running, PE and KE are in phase — both rise and fall together. Efficiency comes from elastic storage, not gravitational exchange.

Section 8

Tuned Track Concept

Developed by Tom McMahon, the tuned track concept applies spring-mass principles to running surfaces. The goal is to create a surface that simultaneously increases speed and reduces injuries.

Traditional Rigid Surface

Very stiff surfaces produce high peak impact forces and greater injury risk.

Compliant Surface

A moderately flexible track provides smoother force application, lower peak impacts, and better energy return. The key is finding the optimal stiffness — neither too rigid nor too soft.

Core Insight

There exists an optimal track stiffness — the "tuned region" — at which running speed is maximized and injury risk is minimized simultaneously.

Section 9

Spring-Dashpot Running Model

McMahon modeled the runner-track system using a spring-dashpot representation. This captures both elastic and damping behavior.

ComponentRepresentsParameter
Leg springElastic behavior of the legK (leg stiffness)
DashpotEnergy dissipation in the legB (damping)
Track springCompliance of the running surfaceKtrack (track stiffness)

The combined runner-track system is treated as a two-degree-of-freedom system, with variables describing both runner motion and track deformation. The analysis calculates the damped natural frequency, which predicts contact time, energy return, and running performance.

Section 10

Equations of Motion

The runner-track system's equations of motion describe how the runner's mass and the track surface interact dynamically. The key output is the damped natural frequency of the combined system.

Why the Natural Frequency Matters

  • Contact time — how long the foot stays on the ground each stride
  • Energy return — how much stored energy is returned usefully
  • Running economy — the overall energetic cost of maintaining speed

When the runner's natural frequency matches the track's properties, energy exchange is optimized — this is the tuned condition.

Section 11

Section 12

Experimental Results

McMahon's experiments on compliant tracks demonstrated improvements in both stride components that contribute to speed.

Faster Stride Frequency

Reduced contact time allowed more steps per second — the spring-like track returned energy more quickly, shortening each stance phase.

Longer Stride Length

Greater rebound energy produced longer strides — runners were propelled further with each push-off.

Combined Effect

Since Speed = Stride Length × Stride Frequency, both improvements act together to increase overall running speed — an unusually clean result where the same intervention improves two independent variables simultaneously.

Section 13

Real-World Impact

McMahon's tuned track research was applied to indoor tracks, including those used at Madison Square Garden. The results were striking on two fronts.

OutcomeResult
Performance improvementRunning times improved by approximately 2–3%
Injury reductionInjury rates reduced by approximately 50%
MechanismLower peak impact forces per stride

A 2–3% improvement in running time is enormous at the elite level — it can be the difference between winning and finishing last in a world-class race.

Section 14

Running Footwear Technology

Researchers applied spring-mass principles to shoes, treating the midsole as a tunable spring element.

Standard Running Shoe

Stores limited elastic energy — typical energy return is around 3.56 J per stride.

Carbon-Fiber Plate Technology

Advanced racing shoes (such as Nike prototypes studied by researchers) combine a stiff carbon-fiber plate with high-energy-return foam. Measured energy return reaches approximately 7.46 J per stride.

Shoe TypeEnergy ReturnEffect
Standard shoe~3.56 JBaseline
Carbon-fiber prototype~7.46 J~4% reduction in energy cost
Performance Gain

A ~4% reduction in running energy cost is transformative at the elite level, explaining the dramatic improvement in marathon world records seen after these shoes were introduced.

Section 15

Prosthetic Limbs

Modern running prosthetics — such as those used by Paralympic athletes — directly apply spring-mass biomechanical principles.

Principle

The prosthetic blade stores energy during landing and releases it during push-off, mimicking the function of the Achilles tendon and foot arch. Proper tuning of blade stiffness can dramatically improve both efficiency and speed.

  • Improved efficiency — energy stored is returned rather than lost
  • Improved speed — more powerful push-off propulsion
  • More natural movement — spring mechanics approximate biological gait
Section 16

Exotendons

An exotendon is an external elastic spring connecting the two legs. It assists the swing phase and leg recovery during running.

Experimental Results

Studies showed that a properly tuned exotendon reduced energy expenditure by approximately 6% during running — a significant metabolic saving from a purely passive mechanical device.

Design Principle

The exotendon stores elastic energy from one leg's deceleration and transfers it to assist the other leg's acceleration — exploiting inter-limb energy exchange.

Section 17

Cost of Transport

The cost of transport is defined as the energy required to move a unit mass a given distance.

Cost of Transport = Energy ÷ Distance

Humans naturally select speeds that minimize their cost of transport. This principle explains:

  • Preferred walking speed — the speed at which the inverted pendulum is most efficient
  • Preferred running speed — the speed at which elastic storage is most effective
  • Gait transitions — humans switch gait when the current gait becomes energetically unfavorable
Section 18

Gait Transitions

Why do humans switch from walking to running? The answer is energy optimization. At higher speeds, walking becomes inefficient and running becomes the more economical choice.

Animal Examples

Many animals transition between multiple gaits as speed increases, each optimized for a different speed range:

KangarooPentapedal → HoppingHopping becomes cheaper at speed due to elastic storage

Section 20

Locomotion in Large Animals

Large animals face unique biomechanical challenges that inform our understanding of running mechanics.

Elephant

Elephants often use a "Groucho Walk" — characterized by bent legs, reduced impact forces, and no clear flight phase. This limits the GRF peak and protects joints, at the cost of not producing a true run.

Tyrannosaurus Rex

Biomechanical analyses suggest that full running may have been mechanically difficult for T. rex, due to the enormous muscle forces that would have been required to support and accelerate such a massive body.

Broader Application

Spring-mass biomechanical principles extend beyond human running to robotics (MIT, Boston Dynamics), rehabilitation engineering, and the study of extinct and living animal locomotion.

Most Important Takeaway

Running is fundamentally a spring-mass process in which muscles and tendons store and return elastic energy. By tuning the stiffness of the body, footwear, prosthetics, or running surfaces, biomechanists can improve performance, reduce injury risk, and better understand how humans and animals move efficiently.
