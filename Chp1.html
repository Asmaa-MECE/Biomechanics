Section 1

What is Biomechanics?

Biomechanics is the science of understanding how living organisms move by applying the principles of physics, mechanics, biology, engineering, computer science, and neuroscience.

It helps explain:

  • How muscles generate force
  • How joints move
  • How humans walk and run
  • How movement disorders occur
  • How prosthetics, robots, and simulations can improve lives
Core Definition

Biomechanics = Biology + Physics + Engineering + Computer Science + Neuroscience, unified around the science of movement.

Section 3

Inspiration — The Fosbury Flop

At the 1968 Mexico City Olympics, Dick Fosbury introduced the Fosbury Flop — a revolutionary backwards high-jump technique that became the global standard.

Why It Was Important

Biomechanical principles explain why it worked:

  • Increase upward velocity by pushing harder against the ground.
  • Rearrange body segments while airborne.
  • Allow the body's center of mass to pass below the bar — even as the body clears it segment by segment.
Lesson

Understanding biomechanics can lead to revolutionary improvements in athletic performance.

Section 4

Why Study Movement?

A. Movement is Essential for Health

Regular movement helps prevent: heart disease, diabetes, obesity, cancer, depression, anxiety, and osteoporosis.

Important Concept

Physical activity acts like "inexpensive medicine" — improving both physical and mental health.

B. Human Movement is Fascinating

Ballet dancers, Olympic athletes, and orchestral musicians demonstrate extraordinary muscle coordination, neural control, and mechanical efficiency. Scientists still do not completely understand how the body performs these tasks so effectively.

C. Improve Performance

Biomechanics helps increase athletic performance, design better equipment (running shoes, speed skates, high-jump techniques), and reduce sports injuries.

D. Treat Movement Disorders

Biomechanics helps understand and manage cerebral palsy, osteoarthritis, Parkinson's disease, paralysis, and spinal cord injuries. Applications include surgery planning, rehabilitation, prosthetics, and exoskeletons.

E. Technology and Creativity

Biomechanics contributes to robotics, computer animation, video games, movies, prosthetic limbs, and wearable devices.

Section 6

Muscles — The Engines of Movement

Muscles behave like both biological motors and springs. They can generate forces up to 10 times body weight. Movement begins when the nervous system sends signals to muscles, triggering contraction.

Section 7

Functional Electrical Stimulation (FES)

Concept: Electrical impulses stimulate nerves and muscles. Used for paralysis, spinal cord injuries, and rehabilitation.

1
Electrical SignalDelivered via implanted or surface electrodes
2
Nerve ActivationSignal propagates along motor nerve
3
Muscle ContractionNeuromuscular junction triggers force production
4
MovementControlled limb motion restored

Allows some people with paralysis to stand, walk, and cycle.

Section 8

The Cybathlon Example

The Cybathlon is a competition where people use advanced technology to perform physical tasks. Mark Muhn, after a spinal cord injury causing paralysis from the chest down, used implanted electrodes, FES, and biomechanical models to win the cycling race — completing 750 metres in 2 min 58 sec.

Lesson

Biomechanics combines engineering, medicine, and human determination to restore mobility.

Section 9

How Movement is Produced — Forward Dynamics

Definition

Forward Dynamics: predicting movement from known muscle activations and forces.

1
Neural CommandBrain and nervous system generate signals
2
Muscle–Tendon DynamicsMuscles receive signals and produce force
3
Musculoskeletal GeometryMuscles pull on bones across joints
4
Skeletal DynamicsForces create acceleration (Newton's 2nd Law)
5
MovementAcceleration creates position and velocity changes
Section 10

How Movement is Studied — Inverse Dynamics

Scientists often work backward: they observe movement and determine what forces created it.

Definition

Inverse Dynamics: determining the forces responsible for an observed movement — working from effect back to cause.

1
Motion CaptureRecord body movement with cameras and reflective markers
2
Inverse KinematicsConvert marker positions into joint angles
3
DifferentiationCalculate velocity and acceleration from position data
4
Inverse DynamicsCalculate joint moments and forces
5
OptimizationEstimate individual muscle forces from net joint moments

Computer Simulations

Section 15

Course Objectives

  1. Understand Major Problems — Identify important biomechanics challenges in health, sport, and robotics.
  2. Apply Engineering Tools — Use mechanics and mathematics to solve movement problems.
  3. Learn Biological Foundations — Understand how living systems produce movement.
  4. Read Research Literature — Interpret biomechanics papers and studies.
  5. Analyze Human Movement — Use experiments and simulations to quantify and understand motion.
Section 16

Prerequisites

Required Knowledge

  • Physics — especially Newton's Laws and mechanics
  • Mathematics — basic calculus: derivatives, integrals, and differential equations

Not Required

Prior knowledge of biology or anatomy is not required. The course teaches all necessary biological concepts from scratch.

Section 17

How to Succeed in the Course

  • Read the Textbook — understand concepts deeply before lectures.
  • Attend Lectures — use lectures to reinforce and connect ideas.
  • Solve Problems — practice applying concepts to numerical problems.
  • Learn OpenSim — open-source software for biomechanical modeling (opensim.stanford.edu).
  • Learn Anatomy — understand bones, muscles, and joint motions.
