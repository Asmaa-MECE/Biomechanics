Section 1

What is Locomotion?

Locomotion is the movement of the body from one location to another. Examples include walking, running, jumping, and hopping.

Biomechanists study locomotion to understand:

  • Energy efficiency
  • Stability and balance
  • Athletic performance
  • Injury prevention
  • Rehabilitation and prosthetics design
Why It Matters

Human locomotion is extraordinarily complex — it involves muscles, tendons, bones, joints, and the nervous system all working in precise coordination.

Section 2

Models of Human Locomotion

Because real human movement is so complex, researchers use simplified mathematical models to understand the core mechanics.

Walking Model — Inverted Pendulum

During walking, the stance leg behaves like a rigid rod. The body's center of mass vaults over it, resembling an upside-down pendulum.

  • Center of mass rises during mid-stance
  • Center of mass falls during double support
  • Energy continuously exchanges between kinetic and potential forms

Running Model — Spring-Mass

Running cannot be described by an inverted pendulum. Instead, the leg acts like a spring:

  • Leg compresses at ground contact — energy is stored
  • Leg recoils during push-off — energy is released
Key Distinction

Walking uses the inverted pendulum; running uses the spring-mass model. The two gaits are mechanically distinct.

Section 4

The Walking Gait Cycle

The gait cycle is defined as the time between two successive contacts of the same foot — for example, right heel strike to the next right heel strike.

Stance Phase (~60%)

The foot remains in contact with the ground. Functions: support body weight, maintain balance, generate propulsion.

Swing Phase (~40%)

The foot moves through the air. Functions: advance the leg forward and prepare for the next ground contact.

Section 5

Double Support

Double support is the brief period when both feet are simultaneously in contact with the ground. It occurs twice during every gait cycle.

Double support:

  • Improves stability during weight transfer
  • Transfers body weight smoothly between limbs
  • Reduces energy loss at step transitions
  • Allows interaction between limbs for efficient propulsion
Key Exam Point

Walking always includes a double support phase. Running does not — it has a flight phase instead.

Section 6

Spatial and Temporal Gait Parameters

These measurements quantitatively describe walking mechanics.

Cadence

Number of steps per minute. Higher cadence generally correlates with faster walking speed.

Step Length

The distance between heel strike of one foot and heel strike of the opposite foot.

Stride Length

The distance between heel strike of one foot and the next heel strike of the same foot. Stride length = 2 × step length.

Step Width

Lateral distance between the feet. Critical for balance and stability assessment.

Toe-Out Angle

The angle between the direction of walking and the foot's orientation. Typical values: 10–15°.

Section 7

Ground Reaction Forces (GRF)

The ground reaction force is the force exerted by the ground on the body. By Newton's Third Law, it is equal and opposite to the force the body exerts on the ground.

A. Vertical GRF

Acts upward, supporting body weight against gravity. During normal walking it produces a characteristic double-hump pattern — two peaks separated by a valley at mid-stance. Peak values typically exceed body weight.

B. Anterior-Posterior GRF

Acts along the direction of travel, controlling walking speed:

  • Early stance: negative (braking) — the body decelerates slightly as the heel strikes
  • Late stance: positive (propulsive) — the foot pushes off to accelerate the body forward

Section 8

Force Plates

Force plates measure ground reaction forces, moments, and the center of pressure beneath the foot during movement.

Requirements of high-quality force plates:

  • Minimal crosstalk — force in one axis must not contaminate readings in other axes
  • Good frequency response — must capture rapid force changes (recommended ≥ 200 Hz)
  • Position independence — readings must remain accurate regardless of where the foot lands on the plate
Specification

Force plates should sample at ≥ 200 Hz to accurately capture the rapid transients in ground reaction force during walking and running.

Section 10

Center of Mass (COM)

The center of mass is the point where the body's total mass is considered concentrated. During walking:

  • COM moves up and down — rising at mid-stance, falling at double support
  • COM moves forward continuously throughout the cycle

Tracking COM is essential for understanding stability, efficiency, and energy expenditure during locomotion.

Section 11

Energy in Walking

Walking is surprisingly energy-efficient. The key reason is the continuous exchange between two forms of mechanical energy.

Potential Energy (PE)

PE = mgh. When the COM rises, potential energy increases.

Kinetic Energy (KE)

KE = ½mv². When the COM moves faster, kinetic energy increases.

Core Principle

During walking, potential and kinetic energy are nearly out of phase — when one rises, the other falls. This interchange reduces the muscular work required.

Section 13

Ballistic Walking Model

Developed by Tom McMahon, this model combines:

  • An inverted pendulum for the stance leg
  • A compound pendulum for the swing leg

The model emphasizes gravity and passive motion — the legs swing largely under gravity's influence. However, it does not fully explain the energy dynamics at step-to-step transitions.

Section 14

Dynamic Walking Model

Developed by Art Kuo and Max Donelan, inspired by the passive-dynamic walking work of Tad McGeer.

Improvements over earlier models:

  • Explains step-to-step transitions and energy loss
  • Describes passive dynamic walking — robots and models that walk without active control
  • Produces more realistic gait mechanics
Significance

The dynamic walking model is one of the most influential modern frameworks for understanding human gait efficiency and the cost of walking.

Section 15

Gait Transitions — Walking to Running

Why do humans spontaneously switch from walking to running at a certain speed?

  • At low speeds, walking is more energetically efficient (inverted pendulum mechanism works well)
  • At high speeds, the inverted pendulum becomes energetically unfavorable — the COM cannot travel fast enough
  • The spring-mass mechanism of running becomes more efficient

Humans naturally transition to running at approximately 2 m/s — the speed at which walking costs more energy than running.

Section 16

Limitations of Simple Locomotion Models

Despite their power, simple models ignore many real structures that contribute to movement:

  • Muscles and their force-generating properties
  • Tendons and elastic energy storage
  • Arms and torso — upper body contributes to balance and momentum
  • Neural control — the nervous system continuously adapts movement
Looking Ahead

Later chapters address these missing structures through muscle models, musculoskeletal geometry, and neural control, building toward full muscle-driven simulations.

Most Important Takeaway

Human walking can be modeled as an inverted pendulum where the COM exchanges kinetic and potential energy efficiently. Ground reaction forces measured by force plates, combined with Newton's laws, allow biomechanists to quantify gait mechanics — while the dynamic walking model explains step-to-step transitions and why humans switch to running at higher speeds.
