Section 1

Why Study Muscles?

Previous chapters introduced mechanical models of locomotion — walking as an inverted pendulum and running as a spring-mass system. These models describe motion beautifully, but they leave one question unanswered.

Exam Point

Earlier models do not explain where forces actually come from. Chapter 4 answers that question: how muscles generate force and movement.

To understand the origin of every ground reaction force, every joint torque, and every stride, biomechanics turns to the muscle itself — from the molecular motor protein up to the whole muscle.

Section 2

The Push-Me–Pull-You Experiment

This classic experiment was conducted by A. V. Hill to demonstrate a fundamental asymmetry in how muscles produce force.

Setup

Two bicycles were connected by a chain. Murdoch Ritchie pedaled forward while Brenda Bigland resisted by pedaling backward against him.

Result

  • Brenda, though smaller and lighter, easily resisted Murdoch's forward pedaling.
  • Murdoch became exhausted from the effort.
  • Brenda used much less metabolic energy to produce her resisting force.

Why?

The outcome reflects a difference between two types of muscle contraction:

Contraction Type Definition
Concentric Muscle shortens while producing force
Eccentric Muscle lengthens while producing force
Key Finding

Eccentric contractions produce greater force and require less metabolic energy than concentric contractions — explaining why Brenda dominated despite being the smaller athlete.

Section 3

Hierarchical Structure of Skeletal Muscle

Muscle is organized across six nested levels, from the microscopic motor protein up to the whole, visible muscle.

6Whole MuscleCollection of fascicles — e.g. biceps, quadriceps, gastrocnemius
5FascicleBundle of muscle fibers with blood vessels, nerves, connective tissue
4Muscle FiberAlso called the myocyte; a single muscle cell, ~50 μm diameter
3MyofibrilA series of thousands of sarcomeres connected end-to-end
2SarcomereThe basic contractile unit, ≈ 2–3 μm long — where force is generated
1MyosinMolecular motor protein producing a few piconewtons (pN) of force directly

Level 4: Muscle Fiber (Myocyte) — Detail

A muscle fiber is a single elongated cell, roughly 50 μm in diameter but several centimeters long. It contains myofibrils, mitochondria, and the sarcoplasmic reticulum, which stores and releases calcium.

Section 4

Sarcomere Structure

The sarcomere is the basic force-producing unit of muscle. Its internal bands and lines define how actin and myosin are arranged.

Component Description
Z-Disc Boundary of the sarcomere
M-Line Center of the sarcomere
A-Band Contains myosin; appears dark
I-Band Contains actin only; appears light
Figure 3.3 — The Sarcomere Structure
Definition

The sarcomere is the basic force-producing unit of muscle, bounded by Z-discs and centered on the M-line.

Section 5

Major Proteins in Muscle

Protein Role
Actin Thin filament; contains binding sites for myosin
Myosin Thick filament; heads attach to actin and produce force
Tropomyosin Blocks actin binding sites when the muscle is relaxed
Troponin Responds to calcium; moves tropomyosin out of the way
Titin Largest protein in the body; elastic spring, stabilizes the sarcomere, produces passive force
Exam Point

Titin is the largest protein in the human body. It behaves as a passive elastic spring within the sarcomere, separate from the active actin–myosin force mechanism.

Section 6

Sliding Filament Theory

Muscles shorten because actin and myosin filaments slide past one another — the filaments themselves do not shorten.

  • Actin slides inward toward the center of the sarcomere.
  • The sarcomere shortens as the Z-discs are pulled closer together.
  • Filament length is unchanged — only the overlap between actin and myosin changes.

Section 7

Cross-Bridge Cycle

The cross-bridge cycle is the molecular mechanism responsible for force generation, powered entirely by ATP.

1
AttachmentMyosin head binds to an actin site.
2
ATP BindingATP attaches to myosin; myosin releases actin.
3
CockingATP is broken down (ATP → ADP + Pi); the myosin head stores energy.
4
ReattachmentMyosin binds to a new actin site further along the filament.
5
Power StrokeMyosin pivots, actin moves, force is produced — movement of about 10 nm.
6
ADP ReleaseADP is released and the cycle repeats.
Energy Source

ATP supplies all energy for the cross-bridge cycle: ATP → ADP + Pi + Energy.

Section 8

Muscle Activation

Muscles cannot activate themselves — activation begins in the Central Nervous System (CNS) and travels down a defined sequence to produce force.

1
BrainInitiates the movement command.
2
Motor NeuronCarries the signal toward the muscle.
3
Acetylcholine ReleaseNeurotransmitter released at the neuromuscular junction.
4
Muscle Membrane ActivationElectrical signal spreads across the muscle fiber.
5
Calcium ReleaseSarcoplasmic reticulum releases calcium into the fiber.
6
Cross-Bridge CyclingActin-myosin interaction begins.
7
Force ProductionThe muscle generates mechanical force.
Section 9

Neuromuscular Junction (NMJ)

The neuromuscular junction is the connection between a motor neuron and a muscle fiber — the site where neural signals are converted into chemical signals.

Neurotransmitter: Acetylcholine (ACh)

  • Carries the neural signal across the synaptic gap.
  • Initiates muscle contraction by triggering electrical activation of the muscle membrane.
Section 10

Role of Calcium

Relaxed Muscle

Tropomyosin blocks the actin binding sites. No contraction can occur.

Activated Muscle

Calcium released
↓
Calcium binds troponin
↓
Troponin changes shape
↓
Tropomyosin moves away
↓
Actin sites exposed
↓
Cross-bridge cycling begins
Exam Point

Calcium is the switch that turns contraction on. It does not directly produce force — it removes the tropomyosin block so myosin can bind actin.

Section 11

Force-Length Relationship

Muscle force depends on muscle length. Active force is produced by actin-myosin interactions, and the amount of overlap between the filaments determines how much force can be generated.

Figure 4.3 — Active force-length curve: ascending limb, plateau, descending limb
4.3
Region What Happens Force
Ascending Limb Length increases; actin-myosin overlap improves Increases
Plateau Optimal overlap Maximum
Descending Limb Muscle stretched too far; overlap decreases Decreases
Optimal Sarcomere Length

Approximately 2.2–2.7 μm, with maximum force produced near 2.7 μm. Total muscle force equals active force plus passive force.

Section 12

Passive Force-Length Relationship

Passive force occurs even without neural activation, arising from non-contractile structures within and around the muscle.

Sources

  • Titin
  • Connective tissue

Characteristics

  • Small at short muscle lengths
  • Increases rapidly during stretch
  • Behaves like a nonlinear spring
Section 13

Force-Velocity Relationship

Discovered by A. V. Hill, this relationship describes how muscle force depends on contraction speed.

Figure 4.4 — Force-velocity curve: eccentric (left) produces highest force, concentric (right) lowest
4.4
Contraction Type Velocity Force
Eccentric Negative (lengthening) Highest — can reach ≈1.5× isometric force
Isometric Zero (no length change) High — maximum steady force
Concentric Positive (shortening) Lowest — decreases as velocity increases
Easy Memory

Fast shortening = less force. Eccentric contractions can reach approximately 1.5× isometric force.

Section 14

Mechanical Power

Power combines force and velocity:

P = F · v
P = Power, F = Force, v = Velocity

Maximum Power

Occurs at approximately F ≈ ⅓Fmax and v ≈ ⅓Vmax — neither at the extreme of force nor of velocity.

Important

Power is zero in two cases: isometric contraction (velocity = 0) and maximum velocity contraction (force ≈ 0).

Section 15

Motor Units

A motor unit is a motor neuron and all of the muscle fibers it controls.

Type Characteristics Examples
Small Motor Units Fine control, low force Eye muscles, finger muscles
Large Motor Units High force, less precision Quadriceps, gastrocnemius
Section 16

Motor Unit Recruitment

The nervous system increases muscle force by recruiting more motor units, following a specific order known as the Size Principle.

1
Small units recruited firstFine, low-force units activate at the start of contraction.
2
Medium units recruited secondIntermediate force units add to the contraction.
3
Large units recruited lastHigh-force units activate only when maximal force is needed.
Benefit

This small-to-large recruitment order produces smooth, graded force production rather than abrupt jumps in force.

Section 17

Rate Coding (Rate Encoding)

Beyond recruiting more motor units, force can also increase by changing the firing frequency of motor neurons.

Firing Frequency Result
Low Individual twitches; low force
Moderate Temporal summation; greater force
High Tetanic contraction; maximum force
Section 18

Electromyography (EMG)

EMG is a technique used to measure the electrical activity of muscles.

What EMG Measures

  • Activation timing — when a muscle turns on and off
  • Relative activation intensity — how strongly a muscle is being driven neurally

Applications

  • Walking analysis
  • Running analysis
  • Rehabilitation
  • Sports performance
Important Limitation

EMG measures electrical activation, not actual muscle force. A highly activated muscle is not necessarily producing maximal force, since force also depends on length and velocity.

Most Important Takeaway

Muscles generate movement through the interaction of actin and myosin within sarcomeres. Force production depends on muscle length, contraction velocity, neural activation, and motor unit recruitment — the biological foundation for every movement analyzed in biomechanics.
